The Person Behind
the Project

45 years of code, 16.5 acres of forest, one automation system.

Eastern Ontario,
Canada

Based on 16.5 acres of mixed forest just south of Ottawa, the property is home to approximately 100 sugar maple trees tapped each spring for syrup production — and to the electronics workshop where SapMaster was designed and built.

The maple operation and the electronics work are not separate hobbies. They are the same project. Every sensor, every float switch, every state machine exists because of what happens in the sugarbush each spring.

The syrup is drawn at the end of the day rather than continuously, after a full day of wood-fired continuous boiling. The result is deep, complex, caramel-forward syrup with a subtle smokiness that sets it apart from commercial production.

The Background

45+ years of BASIC programming — from early microcomputers through BASCOM-AVR to Great Cow BASIC on the MEGA2560 today. The SapMaster codebase originated as an 8,000+ line BASCOM project before migrating to GCB.

PCB design and fabrication is an integral part of the work — not outsourced, not breadboarded. Custom boards designed in DesignSpark and Pulsonix, fabricated through PCBway, installed in DIN rail enclosures in the sugarhouse.

The collaboration network includes Graham (hardware guru, the man who suggested the Schmitt trigger) and Brant (exceptional programmer and code reviewer) — a small team with deep complementary skills.
